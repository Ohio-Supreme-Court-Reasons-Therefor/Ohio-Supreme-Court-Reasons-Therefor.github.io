Ohio Supreme Court "Reasons Therefor" Research

The decisions in all cases in the Supreme Court shall be reported,
together with the reasons therefor.

This page regards research on the clause "together with the reasons therefore" in the Ohio Constitution's Article IV, Section 2(C): "The decisions in all cases in the Supreme Court shall be reported, together with the reasons therefor." Research topics include the history and observance of the clause.

Ohioans added that mandate to report with reasons therefore to Ohio's Constitution in 1925. They then moved the mandate to its own emphatic subsection, 2(C), through the Modern Courts Amendment in 1968.

In 1925, Justice Wanamaker stated the following while dissenting in State, ex. rel Durbin, v. Smith, Secretary of State, 102 Ohio St. 591, 651:

The Constitution of Ohio, as amended in 1912, wisely provided that "The decisions in all cases in the supreme court shall be reported together with the reasons therefor." Prior to that time the majority of the cases, often the big and most embarrassing ones, were decided without any opinion or without any "reasons therefor." The people of Ohio realized that the best test of reasonable judgments was sound "reasons therefor".

King John of England in Ron Anderson's The Signing of the Magna Carta, part of the Ohio Supreme Court's Rule of Law Gallery.

The importance of "reasons therefor" may be traced to the Magna Carta. Signed in 1215, it established the primacy of the "law of the land", which was comprised of judges' written decisions and reasons therefor. That law became known as the common law. Parliament and the first statutory law, the Statute of Merton of 1635, were still decades away.
